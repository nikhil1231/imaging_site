VISION & IMAGING RESEARCH

What does the department do?

Researchers in our department of Computer Vision and Imaging Science
do lots of studies and are greatly involved in the most advanced research areas of modern technologies that are widely used. But there is still much to improve.
Read on to find out more...

3D Scene Reconstruction

The objective of Scene Reconstruction technology is about taking the information from a scene in reality and computing a 3D model of it. The information needed to do this usually comes from several pictures or a video of the scene.
We use 3D sensing techniques with cameras and sensors to get this information. Then certain algorithms are run using this data to connect the images together and form the 3D model.

Recognition

The main goal of many vision technologies is to determine if a specific object is present or specific event is happening in an image. This problem can be broken down into 3 categories: Object classification, where the computer has to recognise a certain object in an image; Identification, where a unique feature of an object is recognized (eg. fingerprints); Detection, where a certain condition needs to be detected by scanning.
Many brilliant algorithms are used to solve these problems, but still even the best of them struggle and perform little better than people. They also struggle with filtered images from many commercial digital cameras.
Applications of this technology include content-based image retrieval, pose estimation (computing the position of an object in an image) and facial recognition.

Cell Phenotype Recognition

Cell Phenotype Recognition is a way of classifying cells. Our UCL department is very interested in this. It's all about observing visible features in a cell called phenotypes. The process compares features in a phenotype to a pre-existing database and labels the cell accordingly. This sounds simple, but it has to process so much data that makes the task impossible for us humans to do manually. That's why we need computers to do it for us.

Motion Analysis

This technology is about processing a sequence of images or a video to calculate the velocity of an object, or the velocity of the camera taking the image. It supports tasks like tracking (following the movement of an object) and optical flow (determining how an object is moving relative to its surroundings). This is useful for all kinds of things: from sports performance to automated vehicles.

Image-Understanding

Image-Understanding Systems, also called IUS, contain three levels. The first is called the Low Level. This level manages ‘primitive’ data like the number of sides of an object, its shapes and position. Next there is the Intermediate Level focusing on the object’s volume and boundaries for example. Then there is the High Level which contains information about the scene as a whole.
This research in IUS is helping to develop techniques like inference and control. Inference means to make a computer deduce new information about an image from the facts already known, while control is the process that decides which technique should be used and when.

Microstructure Imaging with Diffusion MRI

Magnetic Resonance Imaging, commonly known as MRI, is a type of imaging technique mostly for diagnosing medical conditions. Considering our connection with UCLH and the strong history of medical research at UCL this is an area that we are very interested in. The imaging system is being developed to receive data from an MRI scanner and render a 3D model of all kinds of tissues in the human body. We hope that one day this will become a diagnostic technique that will replace invasive surgical procedures, such as biopsies for cancers, with non-invasive imaging.
